Misinformation on Social Media
FAQ's
1. Why is misinformation on social media so dangerous?
Misinformation harms: It can lead to wrong decisions about health, politics, and important events. It can also cause distrust and division in communities. Spreads quickly: Social media is designed for sharing. False information can spread like wildfire, especially when it triggers strong emotions.

2. How do I know if something I see online is fake news or misinformation?
  1. Sensational Headlines: Headlines designed to grab your attention with shocking claims, ALL CAPS, and lots of exclamation points.
  2. Suspicious Sources: Websites with unfamiliar names, strange spellings (.co instead of .com), or those that mimic real news outlets.
  3. Lack of Evidence: Claims without credible sources or data to back them up.
  4. Playing on Emotions: Content that makes you feel overly angry, scared, or excited to the point where you may overlook inaccuracies.

3. What can I do to stop the spread of misinformation?
  1. Fact-Check: Before sharing anything, verify it with trusted sources. See if reputable news sites are reporting the same thing.
  2. Think Critically: Question the source, consider the author's motives, and look for evidence
  3. Speak Up: If you see friends or family sharing misinformation, gently correct them with reliable information. Don't be afraid to call out fake news.
  4. Report: Social media platforms often have ways to report harmful misinformation.
My friend keeps sharing stuff that I think is fake. How do I talk to them about it?
  1. Be Empathetic: Avoid being accusatory or dismissive. Start by understanding their perspective.

  2. Ask Questions: Instead of directly stating something is wrong, ask, "Where did you find this?," "Are there any other sources reporting this?," or "What makes this seem trustworthy?".

  3. Offer Alternatives: Suggest checking out fact-checking websites together. See the Resources page for fact checking websites.
