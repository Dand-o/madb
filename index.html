Misinformation On Social Media.
What is misinformation? Misinformation is false information that gets shared online, even if the person sharing it doesn't know it's false. It's important to remember that it's different from 'fake news'. Fake news is made up on purpose to trick people.

Imagine a rumour at school or work:

Someone starts a rumour (Fake News): Maybe someone makes something up about a teacher or an upcoming event. This made-up story is like fake news. It's information someone creates on purpose to mislead people.

The rumour spreads (Misinformation): Friends hear the rumour and tell others, maybe adding their own spin to it. This is like misinformation. It's when people share something wrong they believe is true, even if it started as a fake story. They're not trying to trick anyone, they just got fooled by the rumour.
What We Do.
Our aim is to educate and help you identify misinformation on social media.

By providing educational resources to help people understand and interpret misinformation, we hope to help prevent the spread of it and help you identify reliable and trustworthy news sources and articles.

In 2021, Safer Internet stated that "half of young people encounter misleading content online on a daily basis".
And, Ofcom reported that "83% of 16-24 year olds use social media for news".

A rise in fake online profiles allows people to spread misinformation, cause harm and create division SWGFL.org

To help combat misinformation, we have created a website with important information and resources to help you identify misinformation online. Once you have read through the information, you can take the quiz at the bottom of the page to test your knowledge.
What to Look For.
1. Think before you share. Misinformation is often attention-grabbing or surprising. This can tempt us to share these types of information online. Make sure to always fact check these links before sharing SaferInternet.org.uk

2. Misinformation can be dangerous. It has been reported that at least 800 people may have died from misinformation during the COVID-19 pandemic. These victims had followed online advice that was being shared, which resembled credible medical information. It is estimated that 5,800 people were hospitilised due to this misinformation on social media too BBC.

3. Don't react to Misinformation. Misinformation is written to create an emotional response and encourage people to share. Take a few minutes before you comment, like or share.

4. Check-in with somebody. Ask somebody you trust to get a second opinion if you are not sure NSPCC.

5. Trust your gut. If you are watching or reading something on social media and it doesn't feel right, it probably isn't. Remember the steps above and check the information before you share it.

Recap
  1. Don't trust everything at first glance. Take a breath and see if reliable news websites are reporting the same story.

  2. Website addresses matter. Look out for strange web addresses with unfamiliar names, lots of hyphens or suspicious websites ending in .tk or .buzz. For example, reliable government websites will usually end in .gov or .gov.uk.

  3. Be skeptical of miracle cures or quick fixes. If something sounds too good to be true, it probably is.

  4. Fake accounts can spread rumours. Unrealistic profile pictures or a lack of personal information might be red flags.

  5. Opinions aren't facts. Just because someone feels strongly about something doesn't make it true.

  6. Think before you share. Consider if the information could be hurtful, if it's meant to be a joke, and most importantly, if it's actually true.

  7. Pay attention to who authored the information. If the author is not listed, it could be a sign that the information is not reliable. If there is an author, check their credentials using a google search.

  8. Now you have read about misinformation online. Try taking the quiz to test your knowledge!
